Background

What grows in Midwest fields during the off-season?

Most cash crops in the U.S. Midwest — corn and soybeans — are grown between April and October. But what happens to these fields during the remaining half of the year?

Some farmers plant winter cover crops (WCC) after harvest, which play a critical role in soil conservation, erosion control, and nutrient management. The challenge: it is extremely difficult to quantify how widely they are adopted, or which fields have them at all.

The Problem

Ground truth is the critical bottleneck

Satellite imagery can detect green vegetation during the off-season — but cannot distinguish cover crops from weeds or volunteer crops. Reliable ground truth is essential, and current approaches fall short in three key ways.

01

Ground truth scarcity

Field surveys are expensive. Many existing datasets are restricted, geographically limited, or not publicly available — creating a major barrier for training scalable models.

02

Limited spatial coverage

Available labels often exist only for certain states, years, or field types — leading to training bias and poor generalization across the diverse Midwest landscape.

03

Label uncertainty

Even available ground truth can contain errors from reporting mistakes, timing mismatches, or inherent ambiguity in defining cover crop presence versus absence.

The WCCSight Pipeline

Google Street View as scalable ground truth

WCCSight introduces a five-step pipeline that uses Google Street View as a low-cost, geographically broad alternative ground truth source — paired with vision-language models for automated annotation at scale.

Crop Sequence Boundaries

The USDA Crop Sequence Boundaries (CSB) dataset identifies fields in corn–soybean rotation at the parcel level. WCCSight uses CSB to restrict sampling to the target population — fields most likely to have adopted winter cover crops after corn or soybean harvest.

Black outlines show all field boundaries; cyan highlights fields flagged as in corn–soy rotation — the subset queried for off-season GSV imagery.

What the models classify

Each street view image is classified into one of three functional groups. These are real Google Street View field photographs used in the project.

Model Performance

Both Gemini and GPT-4o achieve strong zero-shot accuracy

Against ~150 manually labeled ground-truth images (50 per class), both VLMs demonstrate high diagonal accuracy. Neither model ever misclassifies "Others" as "Legume" — the most critical error to avoid in practice.

Both models excel at identifying "Others" (no cover crop) with near-perfect accuracy — the most important class for avoiding false positives. Legume classification shows slightly more confusion, understandable given visual similarity with some broadleaf weeds at certain growth stages. Using inter-model agreement as a confidence filter can further reduce noise.

My Contributions

What I built and contributed to WCCSight

Manual image interpretation

Reviewed thousands of off-season farmland images from Google Street View, identifying winter cover crops and distinguishing vegetation types (cereal, legume, other) from bare soil, crop residue, and weeds to build the labeled training dataset.

GSV–NDVI cross-validation

Developed and ran automated scripts to retrieve paired Sentinel-2 NDVI time-series and street view images for cross-validation — comparing satellite signals with ground-level observations across regions, seasons, and years.

Quality control & labeling consistency

Flagged ambiguous or low-confidence samples for expert review, improving annotation consistency across diverse geographic conditions to produce a cleaner training dataset for downstream deep learning models.

Model evaluation support

Prepared annotated test samples for VLM zero-shot evaluation, contributed to confusion matrix analysis, and helped organize the labeled database structure for downstream classification pipeline development.

Future Directions

What's next for WCCSight

→
Active learning — Prioritize samples with model disagreement or low confidence for expert annotation; fine-tune VLMs specifically for cover crop identification from street-view imagery to reduce zero-shot errors.
→
Geographic expansion — Extend analysis to additional Midwest states and multi-year time series to improve generalization across diverse climates, soil types, and farming practices.
→
Ensemble strategies — Leverage Gemini–GPT inter-model agreement as a confidence signal; explore fine-tuning on high-consensus samples to reduce label noise in the training database.
→
Operational monitoring — Integrate the full WCCSight workflow into scalable, continuously updated cover crop monitoring pipelines for broad Midwest agricultural applications and policy reporting.
